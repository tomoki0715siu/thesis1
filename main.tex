\section{Introduction}

BERT\cite{devlin2019bert} has become one of the most influential pretrained language models in natural language processing. 
By learning contextualized representations from large-scale unlabeled corpora, BERT and related Transformer-based models have achieved strong performance across a wide range of downstream tasks\cite{gardazi2025bert}. 
Beyond their predictive success, these models have also attracted considerable interest from the perspective of interpretability. 
In particular, previous studies have shown that different layers of BERT capture different types of linguistic information, suggesting that internal representations are formed progressively through the model hierarchy\cite{jawahar2019does}.
Understanding such layer-wise representational changes is important because it provides insight into how a model transforms input tokens into task-relevant abstractions. 

Originally introduced in natural language processing, transformer architectures have recently emerged as powerful models in biological and biomedical research\cite{madan2024transformer}.
Despite their strong empirical performance, the internal inference process of these models remains insufficiently understood. In particular, it is still largely unknown what kinds of biologically meaningful features emerge across layers and how such features are related to downstream prediction tasks.
In this study, we focus on tRNA , a small non-coding RNA that plays a central role in translation by carrying amino acids to the ribosome. tRNAis a suitable target for this purpose because its amino acid identity is biologically important, yet the sequence determinants underlying this specificity have not been fully clarified. Although some identity elements have been experimentally identified for specific \ac{tRNA} species, a comprehensive understanding of how amino acid specificity is encoded in sequence information remains limited.

Therefore, this study investigates how a genomic language model forms internal representations across layers through the task of tRNA amino acid classification.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Datasets and Preprocessing}

tRNA sequence data was collected from three publicly available datasets: GtRNAdb\cite{chan2016gtrnadb}, tRNAdb-CE\cite{abe2014trnadb}, and NCBI Datasets\cite{o2024exploring}. The data was converted from FASTA format to CSV files containing sequence, amino acid, domain, and anticodon information.
To improve dataset reliability, duplicated and ambiguous sequences were removed, sequences containing non-ATGC characters were excluded, and all entries were revalidated using tRNAscan-SE. In addition, CD-HIT\cite{fu2012cd} was applied to reduce sequence redundancy and excessive similarity within each label.

\subsection{Data Split}

After preprocessing, the dataset was divided into training, validation, and test sets to ensure independent model training, model selection, and final evaluation. Labels were defined by combining domain and amino acid (e.g., \texttt{bac\_Ala}), and each label was assigned a unique class ID. 
This design was adopted to reduce overreliance on anticodon information alone and to allow the model to capture domain-specific sequence characteristics.

The data were split at a ratio of 64:16:20 for training, validation, and test sets, respectively. A stratified splitting strategy was applied so that not only the label distribution but also the anticodon distribution within each label was preserved as much as possible across subsets. This was intended to reduce sampling bias and to enable a fairer evaluation of generalization performance. Labels for which reliable splitting was difficult were excluded from model training.

\subsection{Model and Training}

We used RiNALMo\cite{penic2025rinalmo}, a pretrained BERT-style Transformer model specialized for RNA sequence analysis, as the backbone model. 
RiNALMo was pretrained on large-scale ncRNA sequences using masked language modeling and was designed to capture general sequence patterns relevant to RNA structure and function. 
In this study, we initialized the model with the publicly available \textit{giga} weights and fine-tuned it for tRNA amino acid classification.

Input sequences were tokenized at the single-nucleotide level. A \texttt{[CLS]} token was added to the beginning of each sequence and an \texttt{[EOS]} token to the end, and the final-layer \texttt{[CLS]} embedding was used as the sequence-level representation for classification. The classification head was a two-layer multilayer perceptron, consisting of \texttt{Linear(1280,256)} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{GELU} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{Linear(256,68)}.

Model performance was evaluated using cross-entropy loss, accuracy, weighted-F1, macro-F1, micro-F1, and top-3 accuracy. In addition, class-wise precision, recall, and F1 scores were examined to identify labels that were difficult to classify, and the most frequent misclassification pairs were also summarized.

\subsection{Layer-wise Representation Analysis}

To examine how internal representations changed across layers, hidden representations were extracted from multiple layers of the fine-tuned model. Both the \texttt{[CLS]} token and sequence-token embeddings were analyzed. The \texttt{[CLS]} representation was used to examine how sequence-level information was organized across layers, whereas sequence-token embeddings were used to explore whether biologically relevant features became separable at the token level.

For visualization, UMAP was applied to embeddings from selected layers. The resulting low-dimensional representations were inspected with respect to domain, amino acid, tRNA secondary-structure category, and nucleotide identity. In addition, centered cosine similarity was computed for \texttt{[CLS]} embeddings to evaluate how samples were distributed relative to the global center of the embedding space across layers. This measure was adopted to reduce the effect of anisotropy that is often discussed in BERT-type representations \cite{su2021whitening}.

The classification model achieved strong overall performance on the test set, with an accuracy of 0.9294, a micro-F1 score of 0.9294, and a weighted-F1 score of 0.9286. Although the macro-F1 score was lower (0.8582), it still indicates that the model maintained relatively broad classification ability despite class imbalance. In addition, the top-3 accuracy reached 0.9947, showing that the correct class was included among the top three predictions in almost all cases.

A qualitative inspection of the predictions further suggested that many errors preserved the amino acid label while confusing only the domain. Overall, these results indicate that the model captured biologically meaningful features relevant to amino acid classification and provided a sufficiently reliable basis for the subsequent layer-wise representation analysis.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Classification Model}

The classification model achieved sufficiently strong performance to support the subsequent representation analysis. In particular, the high top-3 accuracy suggests that even when the top-1 prediction was incorrect, the model often captured features relevant to the correct amino acid class. This implies that amino-acid-related information was learned more robustly than some other aspects of the classification task.

At the same time, classification performance was not uniform across labels. Lower performance on some classes is likely attributable to class imbalance and the limited number of samples available for rare labels. When training data are highly uneven, the optimization process tends to favor majority classes, while minority classes may not provide enough examples for stable class-specific feature learning.

A notable tendency in the misclassification patterns was that many errors preserved the amino acid label while confusing only the domain. This suggests that the model captured major sequence determinants associated with amino acid identity, whereas domain-discriminative features were relatively more difficult to learn. Such errors may reflect not only limitations of the model, but also the biological complexity of tRNA evolution, in which sequence features do not always align cleanly with present taxonomic categories.

\subsection{Layer-wise Representation Analysis}
\label{subsec:internal_analysis_discussion}

The layer-wise analyses suggest that the model did not form biologically meaningful representations in a uniform manner across depth. Instead, different types of information appeared to become salient at different stages of the model. Although only selected layers were examined in the present study, the observed patterns indicate a structured transition in both sequence-level and token-level representations. 

\paragraph{CLS-level representations.}
At the sequence level, the CLS representation appeared to change from a relatively undifferentiated state to a more organized space reflecting biologically relevant labels. In the shallow layer, no clear separation by domain or amino acid was observed, suggesting that the CLS token had not yet acquired strongly label-dependent structure. In contrast, the later layers showed clearer organization, first with stronger domain-related structure and then with increased similarity among samples sharing the same amino acid across domains. This pattern suggests that the sequence-level representation was progressively reorganized from a generic embedding space toward a representation more directly aligned with the classification objective. 

One possible interpretation is that domain-level discrimination emerges earlier because it corresponds to a coarser and lower-dimensional partition of the label space, whereas amino-acid-level discrimination requires finer separation within each domain. From an optimization perspective, such coarse structure may be easier to form at intermediate stages of the model, while deeper layers may increasingly refine the representation toward the final class decision. 

\paragraph{Token-level representations.}
At the token level, a different but complementary pattern was observed. The selected layers suggest a transition from nucleotide-centered organization to structure-related organization and then to domain-related substructure within the structural clusters. In Layer 5, token embeddings were mainly grouped by nucleotide identity. In Layer 10, secondary-structure-related separation became more apparent while nucleotide-level grouping was still preserved. By Layer 20, nucleotide-based grouping weakened, whereas structure-driven clustering became dominant, and domain-related subdivision became visible within these structural groups. 

This pattern suggests that local token identity was not discarded immediately, but was gradually reorganized into higher-order biological features. In other words, the model appears to first encode base-level distinctions, then form representations related to structural roles, and finally incorporate broader biological context such as domain. Such a transition is biologically plausible because nucleotide identity is directly available at the input level, whereas structural and domain-level information must be inferred from relationships among tokens. 

\paragraph{Relationship between CLS and token representations.}
Taken together, the CLS-level and token-level results suggest that the final sequence-level representation is built on progressive transformations occurring at the token level. The token analysis indicates that structural organization emerges before clear domain-level subdivision, whereas the CLS analysis shows that domain-level organization becomes salient before stronger amino-acid-centered grouping appears in the final sequence representation. This contrast implies that the model may first organize local token embeddings according to structural and domain-relevant information and then aggregate them into a CLS representation that is increasingly optimized for the downstream classification task. 

\paragraph{Implications.}
These findings are broadly consistent with the view that BERT-type models develop different levels of abstraction across layers, while also suggesting that, in a biological language model, the content of this progression may reflect biologically meaningful hierarchy in the input sequences. In the present case, the observed transition from nucleotide identity to structural organization and then to domain- or amino-acid-related sequence-level structure indicates that the model did not simply memorize surface patterns, but instead reorganized sequence information into increasingly task-relevant biological representations.
